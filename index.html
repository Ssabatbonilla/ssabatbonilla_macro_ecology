Research identity

Reading resilience through functional pathways

My work sits at the interface of community ecology, ecosystem function, and applied management. I treat macroinvertebrates not as a checklist, but as living recorders of disturbance, recovery, and connection. By combining structural metrics (who is there) with functional indicators (what work they are doing) and trait-based frameworks, I map how ecosystems retain or rebuild function under stress — and where that capacity is eroding.

Functional indicators
Building biomass- and trait-based metrics that translate community change into process-level signals of ecosystem performance and resilience.
Disturbance gradients
Using continuous land-use, hydrologic, and restoration gradients to detect breakpoints, nonlinear responses, and resilience debts missed by pass/fail indices.
Ecological memory
Quantifying biological legacies, redundancy, and connectivity that encode recovery potential — the “memory” that lets streams reorganize after disturbance.
Headwater-to-coast linkages
Tracking how local trait composition and ecosystem function propagate downstream, connecting upland decisions to estuarine and coastal outcomes.

Current work

Projects shaping this program

Together, these efforts form a single arc: diagnose how disturbance reshapes function, test whether functional indicators outperform legacy metrics, and turn that knowledge into tools co-designed with agencies, NGOs, and communities.

Synthesis
Do conservation practices deliver biological recovery?
Systematic synthesis of stream macroinvertebrate responses to management practices across regions and land-use contexts, highlighting when responses are weak, lagged, or contingent on functional metrics.
Gradient fieldwork
Functional vs structural indicators along land-use gradients
Multi-region survey of 90+ streams testing trait-weighted biomass and production against standard indices to detect stress, refuge, and recovery trajectories.
Traits & plasticity
How portable are trait-based tools?
Evaluating trait plasticity, fuzzy coding, and regional species pools to understand when functional indicators transfer, and when they mislead.
Open workflows
Reproducible macroinvertebrate assessment
Curated, analysis-ready datasets and scripts for macroinvertebrate metrics, trait CWMs, and breakpoint models, designed for adoption by monitoring programs.
Citizen & community science
Salt, flow, and connectivity
Bilingual outreach and monitoring efforts that connect chloride, habitat change, and macroinvertebrate indicators to lived water issues.
Comparative networks
From temperate rivers to tropical catchments
Collaborative projects extending functional resilience frameworks across climates, testing which pathways are universal and which are place-dependent.
Future trajectory

Building a freshwater resilience lab

The long-term goal is a lab that reads rivers as dynamic, memory-rich systems and trains students to turn that understanding into restoration and policy tools.

Now
Finalize synthesis and gradient studies; publish open datasets and code that embed functional indicators in accessible workflows.
Next 3–5 years
Launch comparative projects at wetland–stream interfaces, stormwater networks, agricultural mosaics and estuarine gateways to map functional pathways of recovery and ecological memory.
Beyond
Grow an open, bilingual research group that connects agencies, NGOs, and communities across regions—from headwaters to coasts and temperate to tropical systems— around trait-based indicators of resilience and restoration success.
Teaching & mentoring

Translating complexity into tools students can use

My teaching is an extension of my science: experiential, quantitative, and grounded in care. I design spaces where students see real data, real organisms, and real decisions — and see themselves as capable of shaping all three.

  • Bilingual Freshwater Biomonitoring courses linking field sampling, R-based analysis, and cross-cultural collaboration.
  • Aquatic entomology & biomonitoring labs focused on rigorous ID, QA/QC, and trait-based thinking.
  • Quantitative ecology modules: gradients, GLMMs, ordinations, breakpoints, and visualization using open workflows.
  • Mentorship centered on first-gen, international, and historically excluded scientists, emphasizing confidence with quantitative tools.
  • Assignments that move from observation to explanation to communication: turning code and figures into narratives for managers and communities.
Outreach & service

Science with communities, not just about them

I stay active in professional societies and outreach efforts that align freshwater science with equity, accessibility, and on-the-ground decision-making.

  • Mentor and organizer in programs supporting emerging freshwater scientists, including Spanish-language spaces.
  • Community events and citizen-science projects that use macroinvertebrates to tell stories about watershed health.
  • Development of bilingual guides and open materials that demystify indices, traits, and resilience for practitioners and students.
  • Service roles that bridge academic research with restoration planning and environmental justice conversations.
