Research program

Functional pathways of resilience in freshwater ecosystems

My research traces how freshwater ecosystems absorb disturbance, reorganize, or tip into new states. I integrate community and ecosystem ecology to link who is present, what they do, and how energy and materials move from headwaters to coasts. Across projects, three pillars structure this program.

1. Functional indicators
Developing trait-, biomass-, and production-based metrics that translate macroinvertebrate communities into mechanistic signals of ecosystem performance, sensitivity, and recovery.
2. Ecological memory
Quantifying biological legacies, redundancy, and connectivity that store “recovery plans” in organisms, habitats, and landscapes — and diagnosing when memory is eroding.
3. Headwater–coast connectivity
Testing how local functional structure and management decisions propagate downstream to influence river networks, estuaries, and coastal resilience.

This framework has grown from comparative work in forests, wetlands, coastal systems, and river networks, and now anchors a trajectory toward an open, collaborative freshwater resilience lab connecting federal agencies, NGOs, tribal nations, and universities across regions.

Project constellation

Past, present & emerging work (as one arc)

Each project is a node in a single storyline: diagnosing how disturbance reshapes function, testing whether functional indicators outperform legacy metrics, and scaling that knowledge into tools and collaborations that guide restoration.

Foundations · Terrestrial & coastal
Legacies and alternative states
Early work on invasive earthworms, forest pests, wetlands, and shorebirds showed how legacy land use restructures communities and functional trait space — seeding my focus on disturbance histories and ecological memory.
Current · Regional synthesis
Do management practices deliver resilience?
Lead-author synthesis of stream macroinvertebrate responses to conservation and restoration practices across multiple regions, asking when and where functional metrics reveal benefits obscured by traditional indices.
Current · Gradient ecology
Functional vs structural change along land-use gradients
Multi-province fieldwork and long-term datasets testing trait-weighted biomass and production as early-warning indicators across agricultural and mixed-use catchments.
Current · Big-data collaboration
Continental trait & flow relationships
Collaborative use of national monitoring and observatory data to link hydrologic variability, functional diversity, and resilience signatures at large scales.
Current · Open science
Reproducible macroinvertebrate workflows
Peer-reviewed data releases and analysis pipelines that make trait-based bioassessment and resilience diagnostics transparent and reusable for partners.
Emerging · Headwaters → coasts
Functional linkages across the freshwater continuum
Designing comparative projects that connect upland interventions, riverine communities, and coastal processes to evaluate how local decisions scale downstream.
Future trajectory

Toward an open, bilingual freshwater resilience lab

The next decade of my work is organized around building a lab that treats restoration as an experiment in rebuilding resilience — and trains students and partners to read those experiments in real time.

Now
Consolidate synthesis and gradient projects; publish trait- and biomass-based indicators; expand open datasets and scripts that agencies and NGOs can adopt without steep technical barriers.
3–5 years
Launch comparative field experiments at wetland–stream interfaces, stormwater networks, agricultural mosaics and estuarine transitions; explicitly test ecological memory, functional redundancy, and alternative pathways of recovery.
Beyond
Grow a collaborative, bilingual research group working across temperate and tropical systems; connect partners and students through shared trait databases, code, and co-designed monitoring frameworks that evaluate restoration outcomes through functional pathways of resilience.
Teaching & mentoring

Helping students read rivers and data with confidence

My teaching translates complexity into usable understanding. I design bilingual, field- and data-rich experiences where students move from curiosity to code to communication, and where inclusion is built into the structure, not bolted on.

  • Courses and modules in freshwater biomonitoring, aquatic entomology, disturbance ecology, and quantitative ecology using authentic datasets.
  • Scaffolded, inquiry-based projects that carry students from sampling design to trait-based analysis, visualization, and policy-relevant storytelling.
  • Emphasis on open tools (R/Python, Git-based workflows) and transparent expectations to demystify quantitative work.
  • Mentorship shaped by first-gen and multilingual experience: centering belonging, confidence with methods, and pathways into research careers.
  • Collaborative, cross-cultural teaching (English/Spanish) that frames freshwater science within broader social and environmental justice contexts.
Outreach, service & collaborations

Science with communities and partners

My work depends on and contributes to networks of practitioners, agencies, labs, and community groups. I see every collaboration as both research and training infrastructure.

  • Mentor for programs supporting emerging freshwater scientists, including underrepresented and international students.
  • Engagement in community science events and stream-focused outreach that use macroinvertebrates to decode watershed health.
  • Co-development of bilingual guides and workshops on trait-based indicators, ecological memory, and restoration monitoring.
  • Partnerships with federal, state, and non-profit groups to align functional metrics with restoration goals and environmental policy.
