Research identity

Linking structure, function, and landscape to assess resilience

I study how macroinvertebrate communities mediate energy flow, nutrient cycling, and recovery across human-altered landscapes. By combining taxonomic structure with trait-weighted biomass and production metrics, my work moves beyond pass/fail impairment scores to quantify how and how fast freshwater ecosystems reorganize from headwaters to coasts.

1. Functional indicators
I test whether body size, voltinism, habitat use, and trophic traits—weighted by biomass— better capture disturbance and recovery than traditional richness and index scores.
2. Gradient frameworks
I use land-use and management gradients (e.g., BMPs, urbanization, flow alteration) to detect breakpoints, lags, and context dependence in biological responses.
3. Cross-scale synthesis
I integrate site-level studies with regional and national datasets to compare resilience across ecoregions, physiographic provinces, and river networks.
4. Applied collaboration
My projects are co-designed with agencies, NGOs, and communities to generate indicators that are biologically rigorous, implementable, and policy-relevant.

Future trajectory

Where this program of research is headed

My next steps build a coherent research program that treats macroinvertebrates as quantitative sensors of resilience across gradients of land use, climate, and restoration.

Near term
Expand trait-weighted and biomass-based indicators to multi-region datasets, benchmarking against agency indices to identify when functional metrics add power.
Next 3–5 years
Lead comparative field experiments at wetland–stream interfaces, stormwater and agricultural networks, and estuarine ecotones to quantify pathways of energy and material transfer governing recovery.
Beyond 5 years
Build an open, bilingual freshwater resilience lab that links students, agencies, and communities, deploying trait-based tools and reproducible workflows from headwaters to coasts.
Teaching & mentoring

Training scientists to read rivers differently

My teaching connects field natural history, quantitative tools, and inclusive pedagogy. I design courses and mentoring structures that bring students into real datasets and real decisions.

  • Aquatic entomology & biomonitoring: taxa ID, indices, traits, and QC workflows.
  • Quantitative ecology: gradient analysis, mixed models, trait-based ordinations.
  • Reproducible science: open scripts, version control, bilingual documentation.
  • Student-centered research projects from field sampling to policy translation.
  • Mentoring focused on belonging, co-authorship, and professional visibility.
  • Bilingual and cross-cultural teaching across North American and Latinx contexts.
